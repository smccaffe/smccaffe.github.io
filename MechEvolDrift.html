9  Genetic Drift

Recall from our discussion on the how selection works Chapter 4 in that selection is not random, but it does act on random mutations to cause evolution. Selection leads to adaptation by increasing the frequency of preexisting variants in a population that allow an individual to survive or reproduce better than others in the population that lack that variant. We will explore selection in a later chapter ?sec-mechselec. But before we do, we will be discussing another very important mechanism leading to evolution called genetic drift (or just drift). Drift is a non-selective sorting process that is random or stochastic in nature. It can cause evolution, but not adaptation.

Recall in our discusion on HW equilibrium that one of the assumptions was an infinite population size. Clearly this is an unrealistic assumption in the real world. While many species on the planet do indeed have very large populations (in the order of millions and billions and even trillions), seemingly infinite in population size, others are comparatively rare. In fact, most Eukaryotic species have finite population sizes, and some quite small indeed. For example, the Northern Right Whale population off New England is currently estimated to be at around 370 individuals based on recent surveys, with very little hope of ever returning to its historical pre-whaling sizes.

Though the cause of such a small population size in Right Whales is due to us (anthropogenic causes), some rare species, usually with with historically restricted distributions, have naturally low populations sizes (Figure 9.1). Species with small or declining population sizes are the focus of conservation biology, which applies evolutionary principles to develop strategies for population management.

9.1 What is genetic drift?

When population sizes are finite—and especially when they are small—random chance affects evolutionary dynamics. These stochastic changes in allele frequencies across generations is what we refer to as genetic drift. While selection is differential reproductive success caused by differential performance of variants, genetic drift is differential reproductive success that just happens. In contrast to selection, which tends to increase average fitness across generations, genetic drift does not lead to adaptation. Due to the random nature of genetic drift, populations subject to it evolve on distinct trajectories. So, if you re-run simulations allowing for drift with the same parameters, you will get a unique evolutionary path every single time1. The random nature of drift assures that no evolutionary trajectory is like another.

At the most basic level, evolution by genetic drift happens as a consequence of sampling error across generations. If you imagine a locus with two alleles (\(A\) and \(a\)) of equal frequency (\(p=q=0.5\)) in a population with an infinite population size, the theoretically predicted allele frequencies under Hardy-Weinberg conditions in the next and subsequent generations will of course remain at \(p=q=0.5\). However, if the population sizze is finite, chance might cause significant deviations from theoretical expectations. Every individual in the population essentially has a 50% chance to inherit the \(A\)-allele on its first chromosome, and a 50% chance to inherit the \(A\)-allele on its second chromosome. In other words, the genotype an individual inherits in absence of selection is equivalent to two coin tosses (one for each allele inherited), where the probability for receiving a particular genotype is dependent on the allele frequencies in the population. We can simulate this manually using a large jar of pennies or M&Ms, but it is much more efficient using statistical software like R2. Without going into any further detail, we can use the rbinom() function to simulate a coin toss or choosing alleles from a population with (\(p=q=0.5\)), setting a with the allele frequency (\(p\)) and the population size (\(N\)) as input variables. So for the Devil’s Hole pupfish (Figure 9.1), with its very low population size of 38, the simulated allele frequency in the next generation is:

N=38
p=0.5
rbinom(1, size=2*N, prob=p)/(2*N)
[1] 0.5394737

If we repeat this simulation 1,000 times, you can see that there can be substantial deviations from the predicted allele frequency of \(p=0.5\) (Figure 9.2). Only about 10% of observations fall within the predicted 0.5-bin, and the frequency of \(A\) can be as low as 0.3 and as high as 0.7 just because of random chance. That is a massive shift in allele frequency across a single generation.

As you know from experience, the number of coin tosses impacts how close a result matches the theoretical predictions. If you toss your coin ten times, you may get tails eight times, which represents a 60% deviation from the theoretical prediction. However, the more often you toss your coin, the closer your overall frequency of tails will get to the predicted 50%. The same principle applies to the effects of genetic drift as a function of population size. When population sizes are small, genetic drift can induce substantial deviations from theoretical predictions, but the effects of drift get smaller as populations size increases. Using the same simulation as above—but with a population size \(N\) of 1,000—reveals that observed allele frequencies align much better with theoretical predictions, with a spread of observed allele frequencies of \(A\) between 0.46 and 0.54 (Figure 9.2).

To summarize, in small populations, and to some extent in large populations as well, chance events produce outcomes which differ from the theoretical expectations based on HW equilibrium (in our HW model, a violation of the assumption of an infinite population size). This produces random discrepancies from expectation over repeated sampling, basically a sort of sampling error. This sampling error in the production of gametes in finite sized populations is what we call genetic drift. Because it is random, it becomes difficult to predict whether the frequency of an allele will increase or decrease in each generation. We can estimate the probabiity of some events happening, but we can not predict exactly what will happen from generation to generation. It is fundamentally a statistical phenomena. The larger the sample size, the greater the chance our sample will follow expectations. In genetics, the larger the sample of gametes (population size), the closer we will get to HW proportions.

Because of this ongoing random change over generations, genetic drift does cause evolution (as defined), but it cannot produce adaptations. Only natural selection can lead to a directed increase in the frequency of adaptive variation in a population. Sometimes it can be difficult to differentiate drift from selection in terms of changes in allele frequencies, and for this reason it is important we understand how drift works, and how it can impact changes in allele frequencies even when selection is working on the same alleles.

9.2 Drift as a mechanism in evolution

There are equations that model the effects of drift, but they can be a little intimidating. In my experience the best way to learn about drift, the effects of population size on drift, and the impact of drift on a population is through computer simulations, which we will be doing in lab. In lab we will set up a series of in silico experiments to address the topics below, but for now, we will just summarize the major points.

9.2.1 Effects of population size

So how exactly does population size impact the strength of genetic drift? As we will see in lab, the smaller the population size, the more effective genetic drift is in changing allele frequencies among generations. Very small populations can experience drastic changes in allele frequencies across generations. Genetic drift can also impact large populations, it just is less effective in terms the extent of change in allele frequency between generations and it takes far longer for its effects to be felt.

9.2.2 Fate of an allele under drift

The fate of any allele in a population under drift is to either become fixed (defined as \(p=1.0\)) or lost (defined as \(p=0.0\)).3 The speed at which this happens will depend on the population size. The smaller the population size, the faster an allele will become fixed or lost. The larger the population, the longer it takes. In very large populations it may take much too long to even detect a change in allele frequencies among generations, let alone the loss or fixation, but given enough patience, it will eventually happen.

9.2.3 Probability of an allele getting fixed by drift

The probabilility that an allele will be fixed (\(p=1.0\)) in a population due to drift is equal to the frequency of that allele in the population at any one generation. Since the frequency of alleles will change randomly over time, then so does the probability that allele will become fixed. Clearly though, as an allele becomes more and more common just by chance, then the probability of it eventually becoming fixed increases as well. When you are really close to the boundaries of allele frequencies (i.e. \(p=0\) and \(p=1.0\)), then you can say with fair amount of certainty what the fate of that allele will be. However, because it is a stochastic process, you could find an allele well on the path to becoming fixed, only to suddenly begin decreasing in frequency and eventually becoming lost. Thus is randomness.

It should go without mention that the probably of an allele being lost in a population is then \((1-p)\) at any one time.

9.2.4 Fate of a new mutation (allele)

If a new neutral mutation (not under the effects of selection) arises in a population of diploid organisms with a population size of \(N\), the frequency of the new allele is \(\frac{1}{2N}\).4 Since each neutral allele drifting to fixation is equal to the allele frequency, then the likelihood that the a new allele gets fixed in a population is \(\frac{1}{2N}\). This means that new alleles are more likely to get fixed in a small population by chance than in a large population. This seems a little counter intuitive. One would think that a large population would tend to harbor more new variation than a small population, but one might think that is not true since the probability of a new mutation getting fixed in a large population is very small, so most new mutations should get lost. The solution to this conundrum is that drift is less effective in a large population, so larger populations should in fact carry a large number of neutral alleles segregating at low frequencies for longer periods of time. They will have a very low probably of becoming fixed.

Effective Population Size

The total population size (census population size) in natural populations is not the same as the effective population size (Ne), which is the size of the breeding population. Effective population size takes into consideration that many individuals that reach adulthood never breed in natural populations. Consequently, effective population size is almost always smaller than the census population size. Effective population size is particularly impacted by deviations from 1:1 sex ratios. In such cases, effective population size can be estimated as

\[\begin{align} N_e = \frac{4N_mN_f}{N_m+N_f} (\#eq:24) \end{align}\]

where Nm is the number of males and Nf the number of females. If you assume census population of 100 with equal sex ratio, Ne is 100. If you assume a sex ratio of 1:9, Ne drops to just 36. Distinguishing between N and Ne is important for conservation biology and many population genetic analyses related to genetic drift and inbreeding. For example, when Ne is significantly smaller than N, the probability of fixation of an allele in response to drift can be much higher than estimated by census population sizes.

Besides sampling error, genetic drift can also have profound impacts on allele frequencies when there are rapid reductions in population size. In general, we distinguish between two scenarios: (1) Population bottlenecks occur when catastrophic events (large-scale wild fires, floods, etc.) drastically reduce the size of a population. In such instances, survival is less dependent on individuals’ traits (that would be selection) than individuals being in the right place at the right time. Hence, the allelic composition of the generation after a bottleneck largely reflects a random subsample of the original population. (2) Founder effects occur when a small subset of a population disperses into a new area and founds a new population. In that case, only a random subset of alleles travels along with the founding individuals. Founder effects are particularly important in island populations, where species expand their range in a step-wise fashion along island chains. This can lead to serial founder effects with continuous loss of genetic diversity (Figure @ref(fig:founder)).
