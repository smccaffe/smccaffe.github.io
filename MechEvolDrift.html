9  Genetic Drift

Recall from our discussion on the how selection works Chapter 4 in that selection is not random, but it does act on random mutations to cause evolution. Selection leads to adaptation by increasing the frequency of preexisting variants in a population that allow an individual to survive or reproduce better than others in the population that lack that variant. We will explore selection in a later chapter ?sec-mechselec. But before we do, we will be discussing another very important mechanism leading to evolution called genetic drift (or just drift). Drift is a non-selective sorting process that is random or stochastic in nature. It can cause evolution, but not adaptation.

Recall in our discusion on HW equilibrium that one of the assumptions was an infinite population size. Clearly this is an unrealistic assumption in the real world. While many species on the planet do indeed have very large populations (in the order of millions and billions and even trillions), seemingly infinite in population size, others are comparatively rare. In fact, most Eukaryotic species have finite population sizes, and some quite small indeed. For example, the Northern Right Whale population off New England is currently estimated to be at around 370 individuals based on recent surveys, with very little hope of ever returning to its historical pre-whaling sizes.

Though the cause of such a small population size in Right Whales is due to us (anthropogenic causes), some rare species, usually with with historically restricted distributions, have naturally low populations sizes (Figure 9.1). Species with small or declining population sizes are the focus of conservation biology, which applies evolutionary principles to develop strategies for population management.

9.1 What is genetic drift?

When population sizes are finite—and especially when they are small—random chance affects evolutionary dynamics. These stochastic changes in allele frequencies across generations is what we refer to as genetic drift. While selection is differential reproductive success caused by differential performance of variants, genetic drift is differential reproductive success that just happens. In contrast to selection, which tends to increase average fitness across generations, genetic drift does not lead to adaptation. Due to the random nature of genetic drift, populations subject to it evolve on distinct trajectories. So, if you re-run simulations allowing for drift with the same parameters, you will get a unique evolutionary path every single time1. The random nature of drift assures that no evolutionary trajectory is like another.

At the most basic level, evolution by genetic drift happens as a consequence of sampling error across generations. If you imagine a locus with two alleles (\(A\) and \(a\)) of equal frequency (\(p=q=0.5\)) in a population with an infinite population size, the theoretically predicted allele frequencies under Hardy-Weinberg conditions in the next and subsequent generations will of course remain at \(p=q=0.5\). However, if the population sizze is finite, chance might cause significant deviations from theoretical expectations. Every individual in the population essentially has a 50% chance to inherit the \(A\)-allele on its first chromosome, and a 50% chance to inherit the \(A\)-allele on its second chromosome. In other words, the genotype an individual inherits in absence of selection is equivalent to two coin tosses (one for each allele inherited), where the probability for receiving a particular genotype is dependent on the allele frequencies in the population. We can simulate this manually using a large jar of pennies or M&Ms, but it is much more efficient using statistical software like R2. Without going into any further detail, we can use the rbinom() function to simulate a coin toss or choosing alleles from a population with (\(p=q=0.5\)), setting a with the allele frequency (\(p\)) and the population size (\(N\)) as input variables. So for the Devil’s Hole pupfish (Figure 9.1), with its very low population size of 38, the simulated allele frequency in the next generation is:

N=38
p=0.5
rbinom(1, size=2*N, prob=p)/(2*N)
[1] 0.5131579

If we repeat this simulation 1,000 times, you can see that there can be substantial deviations from the predicted allele frequency of \(p=0.5\) (Figure 9.2). Only about 10% of observations fall within the predicted 0.5-bin, and the frequency of \(A\) can be as low as 0.3 and as high as 0.7 just because of random chance. That is a massive shift in allele frequency across a single generation.

As you know from experience, the number of coin tosses impacts how close a result matches the theoretical predictions. If you toss your coin ten times, you may get tails eight times, which represents a 60% deviation from the theoretical prediction. However, the more often you toss your coin, the closer your overall frequency of tails will get to the predicted 50%. The same principle applies to the effects of genetic drift as a function of population size. When population sizes are small, genetic drift can induce substantial deviations from theoretical predictions, but the effects of drift get smaller as populations size increases. Using the same simulation as above—but with a population size \(N\) of 1,000—reveals that observed allele frequencies align much better with theoretical predictions, with a spread of observed allele frequencies of \(A\) between 0.46 and 0.54 (Figure 9.2).

To summarize, in small populations, and to some extent in large populations as well, chance events produce outcomes which differ from the theoretical expectations based on HW equilibrium (in our HW model, a violation of the assumption of an infinite population size). This produces random discrepancies from expectation over repeated sampling, basically a sort of sampling error. This sampling error in the production of gametes in finite sized populations is what we call genetic drift. Because it is random, it becomes difficult to predict whether the frequency of an allele will increase or decrease in each generation. We can estimate the probabiity of some events happening, but we can not predict exactly what will happen from generation to generation. It is fundamentally a statistical phenomena. The larger the sample size, the greater the chance our sample will follow expectations. In genetics, the larger the sample of gametes (population size), the closer we will get to HW proportions.

Because of this ongoing random change over generations, genetic drift does cause evolution (as defined), but it cannot produce adaptations. Only natural selection can lead to a directed increase in the frequency of adaptive variation in a population. Sometimes it can be difficult to differentiate drift from selection in terms of changes in allele frequencies, and for this reason it is important we understand how drift works, and how it can impact changes in allele frequencies even when selection is working on the same alleles.

9.2 Drift as a mechanism in evolution

There are equations that model the effects of drift, but they can be a little intimidating. In my experience the best way to learn about drift, the effects of population size on drift, and the impact of drift on a population is through computer simulations, which we will be doing in lab. In lab we will set up a series of in silico experiments to address the topics below, but for now, we will just summarize the major points.

9.2.1 Effects of population size

How exactly does population size impact the strength of genetic drift? As we will see in lab, the smaller the population size, the more effective genetic drift is in changing allele frequencies among generations. Very small populations can experience drastic changes in allele frequencies across generations. Genetic drift can also impact large populations, it just is less effective in terms the extent of change in allele frequency between generations and it takes far longer for its effects to be felt. 9.2.2 Fate of an allele under drift

The fate of any allele in a population under drift is to either become fixed (defined as \(p=1.0\)) or lost (defined as \(p=0.0\)).3 The speed at which this happens will depend on the population size. The smaller the population size, the faster an allele will become fixed or lost. The larger the population, the longer it takes. In very large populations it may take much too long to even detect a change in allele frequencies among generations, let alone the loss or fixation, but given enough patience, it will eventually happen.

9.2.3 Probability of an allele getting fixed by drift

The probability that an allele will be fixed (\(p=1.0\)) in a population due to drift is equal to the frequency of that allele in the population at any one generation. Since the frequency of alleles will change randomly over time, then so does the probability that allele will become fixed. Clearly though, as an allele becomes more and more common just by chance, then the probability of it eventually becoming fixed increases as well. When you are really close to the boundaries of allele frequencies (i.e. \(p=0\) and \(p=1.0\)), then you can say with fair amount of certainty what the fate of that allele will be. However, because it is a stochastic process, you could find an allele well on the path to becoming fixed, only to suddenly begin decreasing in frequency and eventually becoming lost. Thus is randomness.

It should go without mention that the probably of an allele being lost in a population is then \((1-p)\) at any one time.

Probability of an allele getting fixed by drift

9.2.4 Fate of a new mutation (allele)

If a new neutral mutation (not under the effects of selection) arises in a population of diploid organisms with a population size of \(N\), the frequency of the new allele is \(\frac{1}{2N}\).4 Since each neutral allele drifting to fixation is equal to the allele frequency, then the likelihood that the a new allele gets fixed in a population is \(\frac{1}{2N}\). This means that new alleles are more likely to get fixed in a small population by chance than in a large population. This seems a little counter intuitive. One would think that a large population would tend to harbor more new variation than a small population, but one might think that is not true since the probability of a new mutation getting fixed in a large population is very small, so most new mutations should get lost. The solution to this conundrum is that drift is less effective in a large population, so larger populations should in fact carry a large number of neutral alleles segregating at low frequencies for longer periods of time. They will have a very low probably of becoming fixed.

9.2.5 Effects on the rate of evolution

Based on the above, we can estimate the rate of evolution caused by drift. Let’s define the rate of evolution specifically as the rate at which a new allele caused by mutation replaces (substitutes) other alleles already present in the population. Define the mutation rate as \(\mu\). Knowing \(\mu\), we can estimate that the number of new alleles created each generation will be \(2N\mu\). We know that the frequency of a new allele in a population is \(\frac{1}{2N}\). If the probablility that an new mutation (allele) will be fixed is equal to its frequency in a population, then the rate of substitution (when a one allele replaces another) can be calculated as \(2N\mu*\frac{1}{2N} = \mu\). In other words, without selection (i.e. only drift), the rate of substitution should be equal to the mutation rate.

Note that the rate of substitution is independent of population size. However a large population tends to generate and maintain more variation then a small population, and in reality other mechanisms then drift may be acting on new mutations, in particular natural selection. When a mutation appears in a population, it can be deleterious, neutral, or advantageous; what happens to the new allele depends on where it falls along this spectrum.

  • deleterious alleles tend to be eliminated by selection rather quickly

  • neutral alleles (with respect to fitness) can be fixed or lost simply due to chance (drift)

  • advantageous alleles tend to increase in frequency over time, and eventually be fixed over time (though see the chapter on selection models)

We mention this because the relative importance of neutral variation versus variation driven by selection in determining substitution rates is an issue with a long history in the field of evolution, and continues to be controversial to this day. The observation that the substitution rate is equal to the mutation rate is the basis of concept called the molecular clock, a controversial yet very important idea in evolutionary biology. We will come back to this idea of neutral versus adaptive variation and the molecular clock in a later chapter.

9.2.6 Effects on population heterozygosity

Another important consequence of genetic drift is the effect of drift on levels of heterozygosity. The easiest way to think about heterozygosity is the frequency of heterozygous genotypes in a population, which is clearly driven by the frequency of the alleles.

Based on Figure 9.5, it is easy to see that heterozygotes at highest frequency when \(p=0.5\). The frequency of heterozygotes in the population will will decrease as \(p\) gets larger or smaller than \(p=0.5\). We refer to the frequency of heterozygotes as the population heterozygosity. As \(p\) changes, so does the population heterozygosity. In fact, we can estimate the change in heterozygosity from one generation to the next using the following equation:

\[ H_{g+1} = H_{g}\left(1-\frac{1}{2N}\right) \] Generally speaking, under genetic drift the allele frequencies drift up and sometimes down, but eventually they are either fixed or lost in the population. This means that sometimes the population heterozygosity goes up or down, but in the long run heterozygosity decays over time. In other words, under genetic drift the end result is a loss of heterozygosity.

Why is this loss of heterozygosity important? For reasons we will not get into in this text, generally speaking, having genetic variation in a population is a good thing, and heterozygosity is one measure of genetic variation. If you lose heterozygosity in a population, you are losing genetic variation, and evolution depends on variation.

Other reasons why losing heterozygosity is important include:

  • heterosis and overdominance and their role in maintaining variation in a population
  • there is some evidence for a relationship between levels of heterozygosity and fitness; loss of heterozygosity entails an increase in homozygosity, which often leads to reduced fitness due to inbreeding depression and the exposure of deleterious mutants

These concepts are little beyond the scope of this text, though we will touch on them later in the text when discussing selection and conservation genetics.

9.2.7 Effective population size

An important aspect of genetic drift is the concept of effective population size (\(N_e\)). The effective population size is different from the census population size (\(N_c\)), which is an ecological measure. A good way to think about the \(N_e\) is that it is a measure of the breeding population size. It takes into consideration that many individuals that reach adulthood never breed in natural populations. Consequently, effective population size is almost always smaller than the census population size.

Note

A formal definition of the effective population size is the size of an ideal theoretical population that would lose (or have) heterozygosity at the observed rate (or level) in a population. This is not a particularly useful definition even if accurate. An easier way to think about it is because some individuals do not equally contribute to breeding, the effective number of breeders is less then the census size which results in an observed loss of heterozygosity which is greater then expected based on the census population size.

There are a number of reasons why the number of effective breeding individuals would be lower than the census population size \(N_c\), and we have equations to model these effects. The callout below lists a couple of these reasons along with the equations use to define their impact on \(N_e\).

Factor impacting the effective population size

9.2.7.1 Sex ratios

Effective population size is particularly impacted by deviations from 1:1 sex ratios. In such cases, effective population size can be estimated as:

\[ N_e = \frac{4N_mN_f}{N_m+N_f} \] where \(N_m\) is the number of males and \(N_F\) the number of females. If you assume census population of 100 with equal sex ratio, \(N_e = 100\). If you assume a sex ratio of 1:9, \(N_e\) drops to just 36.

9.2.7.2 Population fluctuations

As populations fluctuate in size, \(H\) will decrease substantially while the population goes through “bottleneck”. In this case, the \(N_e\) is dissproportionally effected by small population sizes, and is best modeled as the geometric mean as:

\[ \frac{1}{N_e}=\frac{1}{k}\Big(\frac{1}{N_1}+\frac{1}{N_1}+...\frac{1}{N_k}\Big) \]

9.2.7.3 Variation in fitness

If individuals vary in their reproductive fitness, for example the number of offspring they produce, than the \(N_e\) is modeled as

\[ N_e = \frac{4N-2D}{2+var(k)} \] Where \(k\) is the average number of gametes produced by a member of a population and \(var(k)\) is the variance. As the variance in reproductive success gets larger then random expectations, \(N_e\) gets lower.

9.2.7.4 Population subdivision

As a population gets divided into a number of small somewhat isolated groups, \(N_e\) is influenced by both number of subpopulations and the rate of migration \(m\) among the groups. A low \(m\) results in an \(N_e\) substantially less than \(N_c\). Conversely, as \(m\) increases, \(N_e\) approaches \(N_c\).

Distinguishing between \(N_c\) and \(N_e\) is important for conservation biology and many population genetic analyses related to genetic drift and inbreeding. For example, when \(N_e\) is significantly smaller than \(N_c\) the probability of fixation of an allele in response to drift can be much higher than estimated by census population sizes.

9.2.8 Founder effects and population bottlenecks

Besides sampling error, genetic drift can also have profound impacts on allele frequencies when there are rapid reductions in population size. In general, we distinguish between two scenarios: (1) Population bottlenecks and (2) Founder effects.

Population bottlenecks occur when catastrophic events (large-scale wild fires, floods, etc.) drastically reduce the size of a population. In such instances, survival is less dependent on individuals’ traits (that would be selection) than individuals being in the right place at the right time. Hence, the allelic composition of the generation after a bottleneck largely reflects a random subsample of the original population.

We can see an example simulation of the effects of bottleneck in the results of a simple simulation below. A population with size \(N\) of 5000 individuals undergoes, which results in rether low levels of genetic drift, shows small changes in the frequency of alleles over time. However, when a bottleneck in population size occurs, in the simulation starting in generation 100 the population size \(N\) decreased to 250 individuals (moderately intense drift) for for whatever reason for 10 generations, we see that genetic drift becomes substantially greater with large changes in allele frequencies over time, with consequences to the population remaining far after the bottleneck event.

Effects of a botteneck on the allele frequency changes in a population. Simulations performed using Web PopGen II.

(2) Founder effects occur when a small subset of a population disperses into a new area and founds a new population. In that case, not only is there reduced genetic variation compared to the original population, only a random subset of alleles travels along with the founding individuals. This can result in rapid divergence of these new found populations due to a loss of genetic variation and the formation of new species through a process known as genetic transilience(Templeton 1980). Founder effects are particularly important in island populations, where species expand their range in a step-wise fashion along island chains. This can lead to serial founder effects with continuous loss of genetic diversity (Figure 9.6)).

9.3 Summarizing effects of drift

In summary, genetic drift can have a strong impact on the evolution of populations and species depending on the population size and demographic history. The callout below summarizes the effects of genetic drift as discussed above. In addition, genetic drift can profoundly impact how the other mechanisms of evolution act to change allele frequencies in a population. As we will see in the next couple of chapters, strong genetic drift can even overcome the effects of selection, can cause populations to diverge from each other even in the face of migration, and can lead to some other counter-intuitive results based on expectations from the simple models we will be exploring. In the following chapters we will be exploring how selection and migration impact the change in the frequency of alleles both within and among populations based a simple single gene, two allele model. Once we get a sense of what happens under the assumptions of an infinite population size, we will briefly explore how drift can then confound these model results.

Summarizing effects of drift

Evolution by genetic drift

Summary

Genetic drift is non-adaptive evolutionary change due to stochastic processes. It is more effective in small than large populations, and its effects are compounded over many generations. Ultimately genetic drift leads to the fixation/loss of alleles and a decline in genetic diversity

  1. Allele frequencies fluctuate at random within a population, and eventually one or another allele becomes fixed
  2. Therefore, the genetic variation at a locus declines and is eventually lost. As the frequency of one of the alleles approaches \(1.0\), the frequency of heterozygotes \(2pq\) declines. The rate of decline in heterozygosity if often used as a measure of the rate of genetic drift in a population
  3. At any time, an allele’s probability of fixation equals is frequency at that time, and is not affected or predicted by its previous history of change in frequency
  4. Therefore, populations with the same initial frequency (\(p\)) diverge, and a proportion (\(p\)) of the populations is expected to become fixed for that allele. A proportion \((1-p)\) of the populations becomes fixed for alternative allele.
  5. If an allele has just arisen by mutation, and is represented by only one among the \(2N\) gene copies in a population, its frequency is \(\frac{1}{2N}\), and this is its likelihood of reaching \(p=1\). Clearly, it is more likely to be fixed in a small than a large population. Moreover, if the same mutation arises in each of many demes, each of size \(N\), the mutation should eventually be fixed in a proportion \((\frac{1}{2N})\) of the demes. Similarly, of all the new mutations that arise in a population, a proportion \((\frac{1}{2N})\) should eventually be fixed
  6. Evolution by genetic drift proceeds faster in small than in large populations. In a diploid population, the average time to fixation of a newly arisen neutral allele that does become fixed is \(4N\) generations. This can be quite a long time in large population.
  7. Among a number of initially identical demes (populations) in a metapopulation, the average allele frequency \(p\) does not change, but since the allele frequency in each deme does change, eventually becoming \(0\) or \(1\), the frequency of heterozygotes declines to zero in each deme and in the metapopulation as a whole.
